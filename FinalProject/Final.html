Student Housing Prices: College Park

An analysis of the variety of living options for college students

Off-campus housing prices have been an issue for many students at the University of Maryland. On-campus housing is the alternative solution, but vacancy rates for student housing in College Park are low for upperclassmen.

Even the prices of University apartment prices are high.

There is a high demand for off-campus housing since housing offered on-campus is largely reserved for freshman and sophomores.

Here is a breakdown of off-campus apartment costs:

Graph comparing monthly rent costs between various College Park apartments (credit: Brendan Miller)

The cheapest options for off-campus apartments at the Mazza Grandmarc and College Park Towers, which have four-bedroom units for slightly less than $900 per month. The average price for a four-bedroom unit in Terrapin Row, which has the most convenient location, is around $1,200 per month.

These prices go up for students who do not have three other roommates to live with and are forced to choose two-bedroom or even one-bedroom options. Rents range from around $1,200 to over $1,600 per month in these situations.

The issue has been addressed at City Council meetings in the past and Prince George’s county representatives have discussed affordable housing legislation, but no significant legislation or action has passed in recent years.

This issue does not just involve apartments. There is also a variety of off-campus housing in residential areas, where students have the opportunity to rent a house from landlords.

This option can be more affordable, but it comes with downsides. Landlords in the College park area hold a strong monopoly over renting to tenants. Students are often left to choose off-campus houses for financial reasons but leasing agreements, inspections, city guidelines, and complications with landlords are all common issues.

Students have reported issues with off-campus houses “falling apart.” Issues with lawn upkeep, broken appliances, structural damage or more are common amongst student renters.

Issues with prices are also an issue. Landlords can afford to drive up prices of houses because of a group of students does not want to pay their price, there is likely another group waiting for an available lease

Houses available for rent in College Park. (1st & 2nd credit: Brendan Miller, 3rd source

The standard of living in many off-campus houses is subpar, but there are not a lot of alternative options for students, if they want to remain close to campus. Students forced to commute due to high costs then miss out on the social and educational benefits of residing on or around campus.

The College Park Mayor, Patrick Wojahn, has mentioned being aware of landlords taking advantage of student renters. But the problem still endures. The quality of living would not be acceptable outside of a college town, or the price would be significantly lower.

Underclassmen are not the only students affected by high off-campus housing prices. Graduate students also make up a significant portion of off campus residency. The University of Maryland currently has approximately 10,000 graduate students.

So, what are some alternative options?:

  • Legislation: implementing a price cap on off-campus apartment prices
  • Reinstating the “stabilization program” that existed in the mid 2000’s in College Park which prevented landlords from overcharging for rent
  • “In 2005, the council adopted an ordinance designed to prevent undue rent hikes, although it didn’t include large apartment buildings. It was terminated in 2014, but there’s no reason rent stabilization couldn’t be prioritized again in the future, hopefully with greater protections for all tenants.” -Maya Rosenberg (Diamondback)
  • Program for graduate students relocating to the area that need more options for affordable housing.
  • Inclusionary zoning and rent control options

College Park and the University of Maryland have created a well-rounded and flourishing educational environment. This can be improved and sustained by the implantation of legislation to control housing prices.
